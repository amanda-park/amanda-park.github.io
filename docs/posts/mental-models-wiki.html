Mental Models Wiki

My synthesized list of mental models and cognitive distortions/biases I have learned about in the past few years.
wiki
Author

Amanda Park

Published

July 26, 2024

Note: listed in alphabetical order.

  1. 💵Abundance Mindset - there are plenty of resources, opportunities, and success to go around for everyone to succeed
  2. 🧑‍⚖️Ad Homniem Fallacy - You think you should ignore the person’s claims if you can’t trust them; however, what someone says and why they say it should be judged separately.
  3. Affect Heuristic - You depend on emotions to tell you if something is good or bad, greatly overestimate rewards, and tend to stick to your first impressions.
  4. 🤓Always Being Right - when you cannot be convinced you are wrong and must be right
  5. ⚓Anchoring effect - Your first perception lingers in your mind, affecting later perceptions and decisions
  6. 🗣️Anecdotal Evidence - Using a personal experience or an isolated example instead of a sound argument or compelling evidence
  7. 🍀Apophenia - Coincidences are a routine part of life even the seemingly miraculous ones. Any meaning applied to them comes from your mind.
  8. Appeal to Emotion - Manipulating an emotional response in place of a valid or compelling argument
  9. 👮Argument from Authority - You are more concerned with the validity of the information rather than the person saying it; however, the status and credentials of an individual greatly influence your perception of that individual’s message
  10. 🤷Argument from Ignorance - When you can’t explain something, you think focus on what you can prove; however when you are unsure of something, you are more likely to accept strange explanations
  11. 🛬 Arrival Fallacy - thinking once you achieve a big objective you will experience a lot of happiness
  12. 🗞️Availability Cascade - Tied to our need for social acceptance, collective beliefs gain more plausibility through public repetition
  13. 👀Availability Heuristic - We tend to most easily recall what is salient, important, frequent, and recent; we are far less likely to believe something never seen or heard before
  14. 🔥Backfire Effect - Disproving evidence sometimes has the unwarranted effect of confirming our beliefs
  15. 🎢Bandwagon Thinking - Appealing to popularity or the fact that many people do something as an attempted form of validation
  16. ➗Bayes Theorem - Describes the probability of an event, based on conditions that might be related to the event. For example, suppose one is interested in whether a person has cancer, and knows the person’s age. If cancer is related to age, then, using Bayes’ theorem, information about the person’s age can be used to more accurately assess the probability that they have cancer
  17. 🙏Belief Bias - We judge an argument’s strength not by how strongly it supports the conclusion but how plausible the conclusion is in our minds
  18. ⚫⚪Black and White Thinking - When two alternative states are presented as the only possibilities, when in fact more possibilities exist. Generally extreme, and also known as “all or nothing” or polarized thinking.
  19. 🎬Blockbuster Mental Model - be the first to learn valuable skills that are difficult with hidden benefits that meet the unmet needs of those you serve
  20. 🥱Boredom Syndrome - Most humans have the tendency to need to act, even when their actions are not needed. We also tend to offer solutions even when we do not have knowledge to solve the problem.
  21. 👚Brand Loyalty - You prefer the things you own because you rationalize your past choices to protect your sense of self
  22. 🦋Butterfly Effect - small causes can have large effects
  23. 🙅‍♂️Bystander Effect - The more people witness a person in distress, the less likely it is that any one person will help
  24. 🥶Chilling Effect - The inhibition or discouragement of the legitimate exercise of natural and legal rights by the threat of legal sanction…Outside of the legal context in common usage; any coercion or threat of coercion (or other unpleasantries) can have a chilling effect on a group of people regarding a specific behavior, and often can be statistically measured or be plainly observed.
  25. 🟣Circle of competence - know what you don’t know, as unknown unknowns can wreck decision-making
  26. 😰Confabulation - You think you know when you’re lying to yourself; however, you are often ignorant of your motivations and create fictional narratives to explain your decisions, emotions, and history without realizing.
  27. 🫡Confirmation bias - Looking for data that confirms your current worldview. Being smart makes you more prone to confirmation bias
  28. 🧑‍💼Conformity - It takes little more than an authority figure or social pressure to get you to obey because conformity is a survival instinct
  29. 🗓️Consistency Bias - Unless you consciously keep tabs on your progress, you assume the way you feel now is the way you have always felt
  30. 🎛️Control Fallacy - thinking you have no control when you probably do, or thinking you have a lot of control when you don’t have any
  31. 📈Correlation does not equal causation - just because two events are associated with each other doesn’t mean one causes the other
  32. 🧊Decision inertia - the tendency to continue to do nothing because not acting doesn’t incur any immediate costs
  33. ➕Discounting the Positive - ignoring or invalidating good things/positive experiences that have happened to you
  34. 👥Dunbar’s Number - the cognitive limit to the number of people with whom one can maintain stable social relationships (usually 150)
  35. 😶‍🌫️Dunning-Kruger Effect - people with limited competence in a particular domain overestimate their abilities
  36. 🪟Eisenhower Decision Matrix - what is important is seldom urgent, and what is urgent is seldom important
  37. Emotional Reasoning - judging yourself or your circumstances based on your emotions; just because you feel something, it must be true
  38. ⤴️Exponential Mindset - Instead of thinking incrementally (one small step at a time), thinking exponentially (each step bigger than the one before it
  39. 🦣Extinction Burst - Any time you quit something cold turkey, your brain will make a last-ditch effort to return you to your habit
  40. Fallacy of Change - tying your happiness and success to someone else’s behavior, only happy if they change
  41. Fallacy of Fairness - preemptively think or assume everything must be fair, letting yourself down
  42. 😱Fear of Missing Out (FOMO) - A pervasive apprehension that others might be having rewarding experiences from which one is absent
  43. 😨Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt (FUD) - A disinformation strategy used in sales, marketing, public relations, politics and propaganda. FUD is generally a strategy to influence perception by disseminating negative and dubious or false information and a manifestation of the appeal to fear.
  44. 1️⃣ First principles thinking - separate the underlying ideas or facts from any assumptions based on them
  45. 🔮Fortune Telling - When you predict events will unfold in a particular way, often to avoid trying something difficult
  46. 🕰️Fresh Start Effect - When there’s an event marking a division in time, it gives someone the sense they can start anew with a clean slate and distance themselves from past mistakes or regrets
  47. 🌲Fundamental attribution error (FAE) - causes people to assume that other people’s actions are less affected by their environment than they actually are, and to assume that those actions are more affected by their personality than they actually are
  48. 🎲Gambler’s Fallacy - probabilities of independent events do not change based on the previous outcomes
  49. 🦾Gap vs Gain Thinking - Gap-thinking focuses on what is lacking or missing in a situation, and trying to close that gap. Gain-thinking (aka positive deviance) focuses on what can be gained or improved in a situation; what’s going well and building on that.
  50. 🚪Gate’s Law - Most people overestimate what they can do in one year and underestimate what they can do in ten years
  51. 😠Godwin’s Law - If an online discussion (regardless of topic or scope) goes on long enough, sooner or later someone will compare someone or something to Hitler or Nazism.
  52. 🌱Growth Mindset - “Those with a ‘fixed mindset’ believe that abilities are mostly innate and interpret failure as the lack of necessary basic abilities, while those with a ‘growth mindset’ believe that they can acquire any given ability provided they invest effort or study.” ○ Growth is uncomfortable. And sometimes finding your magic means being uncomfortable in the present, knowing you are investing in long-term satisfaction over short-term comfort.
  53. 🏈Hail Mary - A last-ditch effort with little chance of success
  54. 😇Halo Effect - Person has one positive trait, assume they must have other positive traits. Happens in almost every area in life, and the inverse also applies (one negative trait leads to assuming other negative traits). Generally applies for attractiveness to most people
  55. 🤪Hanlon’s razor - never attribute to malice that which is more easily explained by stupidity
  56. ⬆️High Expectations - aka should statements; holding yourself or others to high expectations of how things should be, what should be done, or how it should be done
  57. 👁️Hindsight Bias - Looking back on a past event and pretending to know how the situation would turn out, even though in the moment you had no idea
  58. Influence of Stress - In the thick of battle, you will not rise to the level of your expectations, but fall to the level of your training.
  59. 💭Introspection Illusion - You think you know why you like the things you like and feel the way you feel; however, the origin of certain emotional states is unavailable to you and when pressed to explain them, you will just make something up.
  60. 🪞Illusion of Transparency - Your subjective experience is not observable and you overestimate how much you telegraph your inner thoughts and emotions
  61. 😐Illusory Superiority - Your subjective experience is not observable and you overestimate how much you telegraph your inner thoughts and emotions
  62. Inertia - an object at rest stays at rest; an object in motion stays in motion
  63. Intention-Behavior Gap - the all-too-common disparity between what we intend to do and what we actually do; announcing one’s goals seems to widen that gap
  64. 🔙Inversion - thinking backwards to remove barriers to success
  65. 🏀Ironic Rebound - suppressing thoughts or cravings can cause you to become obsessed with the thing you’re trying not to think about
  66. 🥇Just Reward Fallacy - your stress, suffering, and turmoil must be justified and rewarded in the end.
  67. 🌍Just World Fallacy - The beneficiaries of good fortune often do nothing to earn it, and bad people often get away with their actions without consequences
  68. 🏷️Labeling - making a judgment about yourself or someone else as a person, rather than seeing the behavior as something the person did that doesn’t define them as an individual
  69. 📉Law of Diminishing Returns - most important real-world results are subject to an eventual decrease of incremental value
  70. 🤏Law of Small Numbers - More extreme outcomes are likely to occur with smaller sample sizes. Always remember your good friend power analysis when conducting statistical experiments!
  71. 👶Learned Helplessness - If you feel you aren’t in control of your destiny, you will give up and accept whatever situation you are in
  72. 🫗Loss Aversion - People strongly prefer avoiding losses to acquiring gains
  73. 🪙Luck Surface Area - When you do something you’re excited about you will naturally pull others into your orbit. And the more people with whom you share your passion, the more who will be pulled into your orbit.
  74. 🪄Magical Volunteerism - assuming we can decide the trajectory of our lives with nothing but our own efforts.
  75. 🔎Magnification and Minimization - Also known as catastrophizing. Exaggerating the importance of shortcomings and problems while minimizing the importance of desirable qualities
  76. 🔨Maslow’s hammer - “if all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail.”
  77. 🗺️ Maps of reality are not reality - they are reductions made at a point in time in order to be useful to us
  78. 🧑‍⚖️Mental Board of Advisors - Asking yourself “what would X do?” in a situation where you’re not sure what to do next
  79. Mental Filter - taking one small event and focuses on it exclusively, filtering out anything else.
  80. ⚠️Mere Urgency Effect - attention is drawn to time-sensitive tasks that are less rewarding than more rewarding tasks that aren’t as urgent.
  81. 🤝Middle Ground Fallacy - a compromise between two opposing solutions is the only solution (think arguments on if the world is flat)
  82. 🔎Mind Reading - When you think someone is going to react in a particular way, or you believe someone is thinking things that they aren’t
  83. ❓Misinformation Effect - Memories are constructed anew each time from whatever information is currently available, which makes them highly permeable to influences from the present
  84. 😰Normalcy Bias - You think your fight or flight instincts kick in and you panic when disaster strikes; however you often behave abnormally and pretend everything is normal in a crisis
  85. 🪒Occam’s Razor - simpler explanations are more likely to be true than complicated ones. “When you hear hoofbeats, think of horses not zebras”
  86. 💲Opportunity Cost - Doing one thing means not being able to do another (no such thing as a free lunch)
  87. Overgeneralization - happens when you make a rule after a single event or a series of coincidences. The words “always” or “never” frequently appear in the sentence.
  88. Paradox of Choice - Eliminating consumer choices can greatly reduce anxiety for shoppers; generally keep options to 6 or fewer
  89. Paradox of tolerance - if a society’s practice of tolerance is inclusive of the intolerant, intolerance will ultimately dominate, eliminating the tolerant and the practice of tolerance with them
  90. 🗓️Parkinson’s Law - Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.
  91. Pareto Principle - roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes. Related: insistence on perfection often prevents implementation of good improvements
  92. 🫵Personalization - blaming yourself, or someone else, for a situation that in reality involved many factors that were out of your control
  93. 🧑‍💼Peter Principle - The selection of a candidate for a position is based on the candidate’s performance in their current role, rather than on abilities relevant to the intended role. Thus, employees only stop being promoted once they can no longer perform effectively, and ‘managers rise to the level of their incompetence’
  94. Planning Fallacy - Underestimating how long it will take to complete a task or project, particularly bigger projects.
  95. 🦜Pollyanna principle - tendency for people to remember pleasant items more accurately than unpleasant ones
  96. 🎁Present Bias - Unable to grasp that what you want will change over time, and what you want now isn’t what you will want later
  97. Priming - Assuming that you know how you’re influenced and how it affects your behavior
  98. ✖️Probabilistic thinking - estimating likelihood of events using probabilities rather than black and white thinking
  99. 📐Pygmalion Effect - higher expectations lead to an increase in performance
  100. 🙈Reactance - We do the opposite of what we’re told, especially when we perceive threats to personal freedoms
  101. Region-Beta Paradox - people underestimate the duration of distress caused by mild annoyances. These situations often go unnoticed and unaddressed, leading to prolonged and potentially greater upset than more acute issues that prompt immediate action.
  102. 😄Relative satisfaction - human happiness is related to the state of the person relative to either their past or their peers, not absolute
  103. 🛢️Running on Dirty Fuel - some people rely on anxiety, anger, and shame to fuel the motivation deficit that other people have naturally
  104. 2️⃣ Second order thinking - thinking past the immediate consequences of a decision into longer term effects.
  105. 💄Self-Enhancement Bias - exaggerating your positive qualities and compare favorably to others (though people who assess themselves accurately tend to be those with mood disorders such as depression, a phenomenon known as “depressive realism.”)
  106. ♿Self-Handicapping - creating conditions for failure ahead of time to protect your ego
  107. Self-Serving Bias - You excuse your failures and see yourself as more successful, more intelligent, and more skilled than you are
  108. Should Statements - always thinking about things that you think you “should” or “must” do. These types of statements can make you feel worried or anxious.
  109. 🍩Simpson’s Paradox - a trend that appears in different groups of data but disappears or reverses when these groups are combined
  110. 🏂Slippery Slope - Asserting that if we allow A to happen, then Z will eventually happen too, therefore A should not happen (related - broken windows theory)
  111. 🏃Social Comparison - Comparing self-worth to others around us. Feeds into the hedonistic treadmill.
  112. 🛋️Social Loafing - Once part of a group, you tend to put in less effort because you know your work will be pooled together with others’.
  113. Social Proof Principle - we decide what’s correct based on what other people think is correct.
  114. 🌌Spacing Effect - learning is greater when studying is spread out over time, as opposed to studying the same amount of time in a single session.
  115. 🔦Spotlight Effect - we tend to overestimate how much attention others pay to us
  116. Status Quo Bias - We tend to prefer things to stay the same; changes from the baseline are considered to be a loss.
  117. Stockdale Paradox - One on hand, need to have hope you can make it, but also need to face your current reality and not fall into wishful thinking
  118. 🥤Straw Man - Giving the impression of refuting an opponent’s argument, while actually refuting an argument that was not advanced by that opponent (alternative is Steel Man, giving the best argument possible the opponent made)
  119. 🌊Sunk Cost - A cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered
  120. 🥇Survivorship Bias - Focusing on those that succeeded only and ignoring those that failed
  121. 🤕Symptom Imperative - When you exchange one chronic pain symptom for another - think of your migraines becoming digestive distress, or your back pain becoming anxiety.
  122. ⚙️Systems Thinking - looking at the world in wholes and relationships rather than break it down into separate parts
  123. 💲Temporal Discounting - people prefer to receive rewards sooner rather than later. In fact, we will often choose a smaller reward sooner over a larger reward later
  124. 🔫Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy - You think you take randomness into account when determining cause and effect; however, you tend to ignore random chance when the results seem meaningful or you want a random event to have a meaningful cause.
  125. 🧠Thought Experiment - devices of the imagination used to investigate the nature of things
  126. 🛢️Tragedy of the Commons - Without some form of regulation, slackers and cheaters will crash economic systems because people don’t want to feel like suckers
  127. 🎾Unforced Error - error in a service or return shot that cannot be attributed to any factor other than poor judgement and execution by the player
  128. 🤷‍♀️What the Heck Effect - we create our own stress as we sabotage our good intentions with ill-chosen behaviors; “I already blew my diet, so I might as well eat this entire chocolate cake.”
